Understanding Port

How Strong Is Port Wine, Really?

By Elias · Douro Valley

Two glasses in, and Port hits differently than the same amount of red wine did an hour earlier. That's not in your head. Port typically sits at 19 to 22% alcohol, compared to around 12 to 14% for a standard table wine, and that difference changes how it should be poured, paired, and paced.

Where the extra strength actually comes from

Port gets fortified with a neutral grape spirit, aguardente, partway through fermentation. That spirit is added specifically to stop fermentation early, but it also pushes the final alcohol level well above what fermentation alone would ever produce. The strength isn't a side effect. It's the entire reason Port can be sweet, age for decades, and survive months in an opened bottle in ways an ordinary wine can't.

Why the serving size is smaller than you'd expect

A standard Port pour is around 60 to 90ml, roughly half a normal glass of wine. That's not stinginess. At double the alcohol strength of table wine, a full glass of Port delivers close to the same amount of alcohol as two full glasses of red. Ports houses have poured it in small glasses for centuries for exactly this reason, long before anyone was formally measuring units.

Does the alcohol content vary between styles?

Slightly, yes. Most Ports land in a fairly tight band, but White Port and some lighter styles occasionally sit a touch lower, closer to 19%, while classic Vintage and Tawny styles tend toward the higher end, around 20 to 22%.

Knowing the number doesn't just help you drink sensibly. It explains something about why Port feels the way it does, warming, slow, meant to be sipped rather than swallowed. That's not a stylistic choice someone made for effect. It's built directly into the strength of the glass in front of you.
